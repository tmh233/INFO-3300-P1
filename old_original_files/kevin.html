How varied is the billboard Hot-100? (YOY)

The billboard hot-100 is arguably the chart when it comes to song popularity in the US. All 52 weeks of the year, a listing of the 100 most played songs gives us insights into what people listen and buy. Over a single year however, how many different songs become 'popular' by this metric? Do these songs come from a plentiful collection of artists, or do only a few big-name-bands dominate the charts? On the left, 56 years of billboard data on unique song/artist counts are charted, and the trends become apparent.

Trends.

  • The 60s saw the most fluent charts. In 1967, songs came in and out, amounting to 827 different songs in the charts for the year. That's more than double the measly 406 different songs that charted in 1994.
  • More artists got a chance to be popular in the old days too. 423 different performers reached the billboard in 1970; last year it was only 346.
  • The charts of the 90s were dominated by only few. The entire decade follows this trend, with only 291 different artists charting in 1992; an all-time low.
